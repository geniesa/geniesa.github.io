Web Presence Improvement Programme

In 2017, the University of Auckland funded this programme in response to a longtime need for a new, accessible, mobile-first, user-centered website. I was the Faculty of Engineering's lead and the current website is (still) representative of most of the work that was done.

Some specifics that happened along the way:
  • I audited the entire suite of faculty websites: there were seven separate websites associated with Engineering, including five departments, which was condensed to one single source of truth.
  • ... which meant thousands of pages became approximately 200 pages by the time the new site was launched.
  • There was a lot of working with people within faculty to figure out their needs, and being in user testing sessions to figure out more people's needs.
  • I was part of a wider Agile team to migrate and rewrite strategy-focused web content. All the rewritten content aligned with new user personas and stories.
  • A couple of us worked overall IA for all faculties, and I came up with the structure for the 'news' frontpage that was adopted by everyone else.
  • Engineering was the first of ten faculties in the university to fully launch a brand new website.
  • I also presented the successful launch to the university’s senior leadership team, which was not terrifying at all
Auckland Programme for Space Systems

I was part of a team of three people scraping our way through this brand new initiative in 2016. There are many horrifying anecdotes including how we came up with the name, how we publicised all this stuff internally with no money, and basically a lot of making it up as we go along. I was the self-proclaimed communications director and alogside making up the entire programme's lineup of printed material with zero turnover time, I also designed, built, and wrote copy for the APSS website.

This programme is still going strong. It attracts over 200 undergraduates each semester who participate for no academic credit. Some students went off to create their own space-related startups. It also became the thing that throttled the university's involvement in the nationwide space agency. The university now offers several postgraduate study options in aerospace engineering (which I had to write copy for). We also launched country’s first-ever university satellite into space in November 2020.

Engineering course viewer

This was one of the final things I've pushed through before leaving faculty, so time was a constraint. There has been a demand for digital course outlines for years. While the university has finally provided that in early 2021, it still didn't fulfill the needs of students and those guiding them in person. This usually involves showing a very confused student a list of tables full of courses that they can and cannot take.

The people wanted their tables and exact information, so I assembled a team of two academics. One guy provided the admin and user need, the other programmed the data scraping parts on Python over a weekend. I taught a software engineering lecturer BootStrap and we threw the course viewer together in two weeks. It isn't super pretty, but it works.

Print audits and refreshes.

Overhauling legacy publications at a large, unchanging institution is no easy feat. A lot of it involves messing with tradition and pushing for change – from which (usually underrepresented) faces and voices you feature, to overall strategy and messaging. The following are some things where I've been involved with at every stage of the production: concept, copy, design, and getting them set up to be printed.

Undergraduate flyers

The state of undergraduate promotional publications in 2015 or so was a mess for a faculty that wants to show off a consistent front. Each department kinda made up their own variants, so everyone had different copy, design, and formatting. Many of these undergraduate flyers were double-sided A4 things folded into three. At their worst they were printed on NORMAL PAPER in someone's office.

My job was to take over and figure out what to do with each one of our five departments and nine specialisations. I took time planning, writing, and prioritising what individual people with their individual opinions wanted to tell their audience. I then created a template for everybody, and we stuck to a single format to keep things looking unified. We got reuse fun textural shots that always came in sets of our photoshoots and didn't have a purpose until then. Pepople were not mad and other marketing folks were impressed and wanted to copy them. That was good. PDF of the befores and afters.

No, I did not forget about me promising pictures of koalas.

Koalas are kinda jerks though. I mean, I'm not really going to spend a bunch of space bagging on koalas.

Reasons why koalas are stupid jerks:
  • You can't expect to get any respect if you sleep 18-22 hours a day, unlike giraffes. SORRY NOT SORRY, KOALAS.
  • They have THE WORST dierary requirements, eating only eucalyptus (UNSUSTAINABLE!)... which is poisonous in large amounts. These plants are super oily, hard to eat, and have a natural sedative effect (HOW CONVENIENT!). Their digestive systems have somehow failed at this whole adaptation thing, so koalas remain TOO SENSITIVE to do anything else while their bodies are breaking this stuff down. So they keep crashing like over-tired Sims.
  • Baby koalas (CUTE? MAYBE.) don't have the correct sort of awkward gut bacteria to break down eucalyptus, and therefore, the natural way of gaining this is OBVIOUSLY to EAT THEIR MOTHERS' POOP. It's not even just any old poop, but some leaky runny variant that involves acquisition via some nuzzling action.
  • When adult koalas can't digest their eucalyptus, their logical solution is to regurgitate whatever's in their mouths so they can chew it all over again (SUSTAINABLE, I GUESS!).
  • They STILL only eat eucalyptus. ONLY eucalyptus.
  • When they ARE awake, they're eating more eucalyptus.
  • They make loud chewy noises when they're eating said eucalyptus.
  • They apparently either smell like eucalyptus, even stronger-smelling eucalyptus, or cough drops.
  • They appear to communicate by screaming demonically.
  • They also spend quite a lot of their 2-4 waking hours a day raping each other.
  • There is enough koala chlamydia to possibly wipe out entire populations.
  • That koala chlamydia can be transmitted to us humans via koala pee.
  • They have smooth, singular lumps for brains because eucalyptus doesn't really contain nutrients.
  • Their brains are also so tiny and smooth that they're unable to perform tasks as PAINFULLY COMPLEX as eating their precious eucalyptus leaves from a flat surface.

Anyway, I hope that was at least a mildly entertaining diversion and that you now no longer want any pictures of koalas from me. For the record, I'm not genuinely passionate about having an anti-koala stance, but maybe now you'll know what sort of content belongs on a high-quality webpage.
